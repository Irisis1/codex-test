# 3.4 Representative spatial distributions of the normalized free-surface response

Section 3.1 quantified the central response curves for the fixed circular-cylinder ring arrays, Section 3.2 examined probe-dependent non-uniformity within the central region, and Section 3.3 considered the front and rear point responses. The purpose of the present section is to use representative two-dimensional field maps to check whether the probe-based response trends are visible in the spatial distribution of the computed free-surface response. The field_abs maps show the incident-amplitude-normalized frequency-domain free-surface response magnitude, and they are therefore interpreted as spatial response-magnitude visualizations rather than time-domain wave-height snapshots.

For the N=8 array, the short-period maps at T = 0.98, 1.00, and 1.02 s provide representative views around the period range where the central response indicators discussed in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 are elevated. Across these selected periods, the field maps show high-response regions near the central part of the ring and changes in the surrounding response distribution. This spatial variation is consistent with the central response enhancement and the probe-dependent central non-uniformity reported from the five central probes. The maps should be read as visual evidence that the point-response trends have a corresponding spatial expression in the two-dimensional response field, without assigning a confirmed modal interpretation to the pattern.

The N=8 field map at T = 1.40 s is included as a longer-period representative case for comparison with the short-period maps. Its role is to show that the normalized free-surface response magnitude is not spatially invariant with period: the distribution around and outside the ring changes when the selected period is moved away from the short-period cases. This comparison provides a qualitative spatial context for the period-dependent probe responses discussed in the preceding subsections, but it does not by itself establish the physical origin of the pattern.

Overall, the representative field_abs maps provide spatial visualization evidence that supports the probe-based trends identified in Sections 3.1-3.3. They are useful for showing where elevated and non-uniform response magnitudes appear in the computational domain for selected periods. However, these field maps should not be used alone to prove modal localization, a labelled wave mechanism, a metamaterial-type effect, a response-reduction claim, or any far-field energy metric. The interpretation is therefore limited to representative spatial distributions of the incident-amplitude-normalized frequency-domain free-surface response magnitude for the specified fixed-body diffraction cases.
